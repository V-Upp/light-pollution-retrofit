Project Background

Analyzing whether existing municipal cobra-head streetlights can be mechanically retrofitted to mitigate upward light pollution while preserving civil roadway safety margins.

↓
Big Bend National Park Reference

Project Origin

In the early September of 2025, a road trip to Big Bend National Park—a designated International Dark Sky Sanctuary—fundamentally shifted our understanding of the nocturnal environment. In the absence of local light interference, the true, uninhibited scale and density of the night sky became completely visible. Returning home to North Texas, the contrast was jarring; the pristine deep-space clarity was entirely replaced by a bleached-out urban skyglow. This stark disparity led to a baseline inquiry: why are we losing our night skies? Our subsequent research identified standard, unshielded cobra-head streetlights driving high volumes of wasted upward light spill as a primary culprit, initiating this investigation into a scalable, mechanical solution.

Environment A: Low-interference sky (West Texas Baseline)
Environment B: Bleached-out urban skyglow (DFW Metroplex)

Experiencing both environments in rapid succession inspired us to investigate whether existing roadway lighting infrastructure could be mechanically optimized rather than entirely replaced.

Primary Atmospheric Anomalies

[01]

Sky Glow

Wasted upward light scattering off atmospheric particles, creating an artificial twilight dome that completely obscures celestial tracking and stellar visibility over metropolitan areas.

[02]

Glare

High-intensity unshielded light shining horizontally into drivers' fields of vision, causing disability glare that reduces visual contrast and compromises vehicle operational safety.

[03]

Light Trespass

Uncontrolled lateral spill light projecting beyond municipal property line boundaries into natural ecosystems and residential windows, disrupting circadian biological rhythms.

Central Research Hypothesis
"Can a removable, low-mass mechanical retrofit successfully reduce upward and lateral spill light from existing cobra-head fixtures without degrading required pavement illuminance profiles?"

Core Investigation Objective

We are systematically investigating whether a non-permanent retrofit housing can minimize peripheral light spill and glare parameters while completely preserving critical roadway visibility and public safety requirements.

Engineering Design Constraints

No Drilling

Zero permanent geometric modifications or structural tapping allowed on the underlying OEM utility fixture to comply with safety liability standards.

Low Cost

Manufacturable via cost-effective processing parameters to remain viable for rapid, large-scale municipal deployment budgets.

Lightweight

Minimal added mass to completely prevent structural weld fatigue or altering the pre-calculated center of gravity on the utility mast arm.

Easy Maintenance

Zero interference with internal terminal electronics housing, quick-release latches, or existing top-mounted photocell functionality.

Universal Compatibility

Form-factor must wrap seamlessly around standard legacy cobra-head housing geometry variants widely deployed in public works.

Roadway Safety

Total preservation of target downward photometric performance and beam patterns as dictated by local Department of Transportation codes.
